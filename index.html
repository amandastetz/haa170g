Town Versus Gown?

Historically, the phrase “the town and gown” has been used to describe the two distinct communities living within a university town.


The “town” being the community’s non-academic residents and the “gown” being all those associated with the university – students, facility, and staff, amongst others.

Fittingly, Town Gown is the name of the report Harvard, MIT, Lesley University, and Cambridge College each release annually to the public to describe what they have been doing for the past year. It also describes what their future plans are regarding topics like land ownership, student and staff statistics, building renovations and construction, and energy consumption.1 Harvard University has called the report a “snapshot of Harvard’s presence in Cambridge”.2

The following data story is inspired by this town-gown relationship as it applies to Harvard University and the communities surrounding it: Harvard Square, greater-Cambridge, Allston, and more. It uses data from various Harvard Town Gown reports, Harvard GIS data, and other open-source databases, to look at what occurs when the “town and gown” relationship becomes one of “town versus gown”.

So, what does town versus gown look like, and why is this relationship relevant? To understand, it is first important to recognize Harvard’s growing physical presence, how it affects the community around it, and the community’s response to it.


As of 2018, Harvard has owned 9.56% of the land in Cambridge and 0.27% in Boston.3

As seen below, Harvard’s presence beyond its main campus has been slowly expanding as well since 2002.

The University currently owns land in Massachusetts, Maine, Connecticut, Washington D.C., and Wyoming. It also maintains an international presence through centers in Italy, Greece, and Turkey. In total, these holdings account for 25.8 million square feet of building space, and 5669 acres of land as of 2021.4

While the university’s expansion may not be negative in-and-of-itself, its expansion does not occur in a vacuum.


The communities Harvard is most immediately expanding into – Cambridge and Allston – are densely populated with little land available, meaning Harvard’s expansion is a zero-sum game: for the university to acquire new property, people, often entire communities, must be driven out. And those that stay, often do not remain for long.

Here the distinction between the town and gown is most apparent, because the residents of these communities the university seeps into do not benefit from its wealth nor the resources it provides internally. Rather, the immediate effects of Harvard’s expansion come in the form of skyrocketing property values and costs of living, which are impossible to keep up with on a stagnant income.5

"In the last 10 years, Cambridge has experienced some of the highest home appreciation rates of any community in the nation."6

Local residents and businesses are effectively priced out of communities they may have spent decades in. Yet, this story is not new.

In fact, Harvard has been in a state of continuous growth for over a century. To secure and maintain its property holdings at this rate, Harvard has drawn from the same playbook for nearly 400 years.

The following sections, showcase the university’s physical expansion, and explain Harvard’s motives for expansion and how its role within the broader community has changed from 1636 to today. Understanding the evolving town-gown relationship will help make sense of the current relationship between Harvard Square, great-Cambridge, Allston, and Harvard.

Early Harvard: 1636 – 1869
The first 230 years of Harvard’s physical presence in Cambridge was almost entirely limited to around twenty substantial buildings.

Almost all of which were contained within what is known as Old Yard today. Near the beginning of the 19th century, the university began its first expansion – 64 acres into the north of Harvard Yard – yet this expansion did not interrupt Cambridge’s own growth.7

In these early years the college maintained a small student body size, around two hundred, and most students lived in private homes off-campus, yet the “town versus gown” relationship still saw its roots in this period.

Notably, it was not the nature of the university expanding that created conflict with the town surrounding it, but rather it was the disposition of the students.

Often, students were called “habitually riotous and arrogant” which in turn “adversely affected the life in the village.”8 This is a distinction from all subsequent time-periods where the town-gown relationship becomes marked by the university’s actions itself.
Eliot: 1870 – 1909
The next distinctive period of growth for the university was during Charles Eliot’s presidency.

Eliot grew undergraduate enrollment to over 2200, graduate enrollment to over 1000, and in doing so was able to practically double the amount of buildings on Harvard’s campus.9 It was during this time that Harvard’s relationship with the local government and residents took shape.

During the 1890s there were talks by the Cambridge government about rolling back the blanketed tax-exemptions Harvard enjoyed on almost every building.

At the time, while educational institutions could not be taxed, new legislation was passed which ended tax-exemption for properties whose income was not used for educational purposes and for properties which were not occupied for educational purposes.10

This was the beginning of a long history of push-and-pull between Harvard and local governments over its tax-exempt status.

During this time, Harvard also forged a symbiotic relationship with the town of Cambridge. Eliot was a strong proponent of the German university model, which believed that a university should only provide classrooms and libraries to its students, not housing or other amenities. As a result, Eliot created a liberalized housing policy, where most Harvard students lived and ate off campus.

This created new demand for private dormitories, apartments, and cafeteria facilities, often run by Cambridge residents. The surge of the student population also strengthened the economy of Harvard Square, driving demand for restaurants, repair shops, and other local businesses.
Lowell: 1910 – 1932
The presidency of Abbott Lawrence Lowell is perhaps the most pivotal period within Harvard’s history of expansion.

The actions of Lowell not only changed the course of the institution’s housing policies but in doing so they created the first major rift between the university and the residents of Harvard Square.

While Eliot’s housing policy may have been beneficial for business owners in the Square, it created immense inequalities in quality of life for Harvard students. Since the quality and location of housing was subject to how much students could afford, poor students were required to live miles away from Harvard Square to obtain affordable housing, while richer students occupied luxury dormitories and dining halls a mere minutes walk from Harvard Yard. This split was not only in living conditions, it fragmented the student body along class lines.

As an attempt to remedy this Lowell established the Housing System as it is known today, where all Harvard students are able to live on campus in shared dormitories, regardless of class affiliation. To make this a reality Lowell underwent one of the most substantial building projects in Harvard’s history, constructing several dormitories along the Charles River in addition to other academic buildings along the Yard.

While the result may have equalized the field for Harvard students, it had detrimental effects on the Cambridge community in two ways. First, all of the residents whose livelihoods were dependent on running housing and other amenities for the students were suddenly put out of business. Second, the means by which Lowell acquired almost all the land along the Charles River were immensely deceptive, if not outright destructive to communities.

From a period of roughly 1902 - 1932, Lowell used proxy real estate agents to secretly buy almost all of the land along the Charles River.11

This area along the river originally housed a large working-class immigrant community. However, it was systematically cleared away through a series of aggressive purchases and persuasion by two Harvard alumni. Residents often did not know they were signing their property over to the university nor that they were being swindled into the lowest prices possible.

This land grab bolstered the university’s wealth at the expense of losing the trust of city residents and the local government as well. During this time, around 1928, when tensions were high the university promised to “pay a sum in lieu of taxes on the properties it might acquire over the next twenty years”,12 marking the beginning of a long history of the university promising “payments in lieu of taxes”, to the city as compensation, otherwise known as PILOT funds.
Pre-Allston: 1933 - 1996
The era between Lowell’s presidency and Harvard in the 21st century was again one of immense expansion.

From 1953 - 1971, during Nathan Pusey’s presidency Harvard entered its most prolific building era during these postwar years, and in doing so was required to negotiate its PILOT payments.13

Three new freshman dormitories and three new upperclassmen Houses along the River were constructed, along with a variety of other academic facilities. These construction projects for the first time added skyscrapers – William James Hall and Mather House – to Harvard’s real estate portfolio.

While this construction occurred the city remained deeply cynical of the university’s intentions and capabilities.14 The community had not forgotten about the secret purchasing that occurred earlier in the century, and as a result, popular imagination characterized the university as no better than a real-estate speculator. In many ways Harvard was just that, prior to Pusey’s presidency the university frequently landbanked properties, meaning it bought real estate with the intention of later demolishing it, which in the eyes of the university meant there was no incentive to upkeep the properties. As a result, Harvard let these buildings decay to the detriment and danger of those inhabiting it.

To assess the university’s image in light of this friction between the town and gown, Pusey organized the creation of a “Committee on the University and the City”, composed of Harvard faculty and staff.

In their report, and much to the town’s dismay, the committee talked of a natural trajectory towards a “Harvardized” future for Cambridge (gentrification).15

The distrust of the university by the town would go on to shape Harvard’s image for those living in the area, and to this day Harvard largely continues to live up to that image.
Post-Allston: 1997 - Today
The Boston Globe called it,
“a stealthy land grab.”

Harvard called it,
“normal.”16

In 1997, Harvard University disclosed that for the previous nine years they had spent nearly $88 million buying 52 acres of land in Allston, Massachusetts behind Harvard Business School and Harvard’s athletic complexes.17 Echoing the actions of almost a century earlier, the process had been conducted secretly under the name of an unaffiliated private real estate company, meaning the university’s identity was unknown to sellers, Allston residents, and local government officials.

Harvard claims to have done so out of necessity, stating that if they went through with the purchases under the university name, the prices would have been inflated beyond their fair market value.18 Allston residents, on the other hand, viewed the terms of the purchases as deception.

Residents did not want the university further encroaching on their community, as it typically translates to a higher cost of living, less resources for residents, and greater gentrification. To mitigate these tensions, Harvard promised to remain “very sensitive to community interests” when it came to deciding the use of said properties.19

It has been 25 years since this Boston Globe article was published, and Harvard now owns approximately ⅓ of the land in Allston.20

This land has since been turned into a Science and Engineering Complex, an Innovation Lab, and Art Lab, along with other academic buildings, and these are just the start. As Harvard rolls out its plan for the remodeling of more of its Allston properties, it claims once again for these projects to be in service of the community.21 However, the reality of the situation is not so simple, especially with mounting opposition from neighborhood groups.

The “Allston Question” is perhaps the most symbolic of Harvard’s contemporary relationship with the communities it inhabits. The remainder of this data story will focus on Harvard’s presence in Allston to outline the current effects of expansion.

The Effects of Harvard in Allston


In 2002 the amount of acreage Harvard held in Allston surpassed that in Cambridge, and this amount has remained significantly greater ever since, with a trajectory to acquire even more. Most of this land remains undeveloped, explaining the lower gross square footage in Allston building properties. However, this amount is also projected to continually increase as Harvard unveils its Allston initiative.

Harvard may continue to expand, but that does not mean it does so without pushback. The effects of Harvard’s expansion have historically affected communities along two axes: culturally and financially. As a result, voices against Harvard focus their attention on gentrification and taxation.



The “Essential Character” of a Community

Gentrification is a term to describe the process by which an area experiences an influx of wealthier people who through renovations to homes and businesses increase the area’s property value and displace its previous inhabitants. As a result, the “essential character” of a community, which is defined by the people and culture within it, changes.

As reports from the Harvard Open Data Project22 reveal, gentrification is a consequence of, and arguably a necessity for, Harvard’s expansion. The Harvard Corporation, who oversees the university’s budget, is incentivized to prioritize the interests of the university over those within the communities it enters. As a result, Harvard reshapes the communities surrounding it.

How does this happen?

Between 2002 and 2021, Harvard increased the amount of square footage it owned in Cambridge by 24%. The result?

In those same years the average home price in Cambridge rose by over 150%, 35 times higher than the national rate of appreciation: 4.27%.23

Given these rates it is no surprise that residents are being priced-out and displaced from their neighborhoods. This runs in stark contrast to Harvard’s continual expansion of the student body. When looking at the steadily rising residential portion of Harvard’s building use, it is clear that while the student housing market flourishes, non-student residents pay the price – something which echoes the struggles residents faced back in the early 1900s when Harvard moved to the on-campus housing system. Today, even residents who do not leave, experience their community’s resources and opportunities retract, as many of the jobs Harvard provides, beyond entry level, do not reflect the communities they serve.24

HOPD has shown that the fundamental characteristics of communities change once Harvard enters them, finding a direct correlation between Harvard’s growth and shrinking populations of low-income people of color.25 With these findings in mind it is no surprise that many Allston residents have a “fear of Harvard just kind of coming in and swallowing the neighborhood.”26 Arguably, the motivation for Harvard entering Allston was due to them exhausting the communities they could displace to make room for more university buildings.

In 2011, the former head of Harvard Real Estate and former Vice President for administration, Sally Zeckhauser, spoke on Harvarding “running out of space” in Cambridge, stating that the university was bumping up against “mature neighborhoods with sophisticated and politically active residents often seeking to preserve things just as they were.”27 The result? A move to an area with less “sophisticated and politically active residents”, a phrase which is largely a “dog-whistle for low-income and people of color.”28 The University determined this was Allston, which explains the beginning of their development plans there in 2013.



Taxation and PILOT

Harvard may exist within various communities but that does not mean it is fundamentally a part of them. In fact, the communities Harvard lies in are often responsible for footing the bill of the university. As non-profit organizations, universities are exempt from paying property taxes,29 even in places like the Boston area, where over 50% of land is tax-exempt due to its non-profit or governmental use.30

The most recent Harvard Town Gown report shows that in 2020 Harvard owned 191.8 acres of land in Cambridge yet only 22.4 – a mere 12% – is taxable.

Why is this a problem? Well, 70% of Boston’s budget and 65% of Cambridge’s budget comes from property taxes.31 As a result, costs of snow removal, road maintenance, police and fire protection, and other local services disproportionately fall on town residents, despite Harvard affiliates benefiting from these services as well.

In an attempt to make up for these loses, in 2011 Cambridge and Boston established a new Payment in Lieu of Taxes (PILOT) program, which asks nonprofit institutions, educational, medical, or cultural, whose property is valued at over $15 million to donate around 25% of their assessed property tax value per year.32

There is even an arrangement called “community benefits” which allows up to half of the institution’s expected contributions to PILOT to be fulfilled through “services that directly benefit City of Boston residents.”33 The purported goal is to create an impact equivalent to the dollar amount that would have been paid.

While in theory this PILOT program sounds strong, the reality is far from it. A major caveat of PILOT is that cities cannot legally require institutions to comply with PILOT payment amounts, since as mentioned these institutions are protected by law as tax-exempt. Hence all contributions are entirely voluntary – which is reflected by Harvard’s participation in PILOT.

As of 2021, Harvard has never paid the full amount of their share, approximately $12.8 million. When they do offer payment, often over half of it is through “community benefits” credit.34 The broad nature of the terms of “community benefits” has allowed the university to create programs with very little direct impact on the Boston communities that need them most.

In 2017 they only paid 53% of their PILOT.35 In 2019, they paid 70%.36

Harvard’s 2020 community benefits summaryreveals the entirety of the Arnold Arboretum’s operating budget, around $11 million, was written as “benefits” to the community.37 In 2019, Harvard also considered offering “$33,000 worth of scholarships to 16 Boston-area high school students, and $221,500 worth of scholarships to five Boston teachers for a degree from the Harvard Graduate School of Education” as benefits for the entirety of Boston.38



Harvard Enterprise Research Campus

Harvard’s expansion into Allston warrants fears for both the culture of the city and its financial stability. Which is why Harvard’s newest building project in Allston, the Enterprise Research Campus (ERC), has already faced immense pushback from community groups.

Since 2002 Harvard has dedicated a significant amount of its building ownership to undeveloped land and developing real estate, and clearly is on no track to stop. The ERC would be one of Harvard’s most ambitious projects yet. The ERC was pitched in 2021 to be “900,000 square feet of mixed-use development consisting of residential, office/lab, hotel, conference center, restaurant, and retail use” located in Allston between Western Avenue and Cambridge Street.39 The area would be enveloped by over two acres of public open space with greenways and other sustainable architectures.

As Harvard remarks, the ERC is “intended to complement the vibrancy, diversity, and creativity of the Allston community”40 which is why Harvard has pledged to actions such as: allocate 20% of the ERC as publicly accessible open space; donate land for affordable housing; ensure that 20% of the housing units in the ERC would be income restricted; provide up to $10 million over five years for affordable-housing creation and preservation; ensure that a quarter of retail areas in future ERC projects will be reserved for local, small, and/or minority or women-owned businesses; and provide $1.05 million in new funding for workforce education programs in professional skills aligned with future ERC job opportunities, amongst others.41



A recreation of the map of the ERC by Harvard University Planning and Design. It depicts the Allston Planning Area: the 36-acre Enterprise Research Campus, in purple; the 14-acre parcel where development is pending, in red.

Despite large overlap in demands by both Harvard and Allston residents for transportation, affordable housing, public spaces, and professional development, neighborhood groups like Coalition for a Just Allston-Brighton remain wary. They voice a stronger desire for a “more comprehensive approach to development planning and more engagement by Harvard – as opposed to independent developers – in broad community-planning efforts.”42

A clear compromise is further muddled through Harvard’s desire, as stated in a footnote on their pledge, that this ERC commercial space may be later converted into campus space.

Viewpoints &
Moving Forward

It is clear through nearly 400 years of history, expansion is something deeply intertwined with the university, and something that has consistently detrimental first the residents of Harvard Square, then of greater-Cambridge, and now of Allston.


As a result, Harvard has used similar tactics throughout the years to acquire and maintain its property. The relationship between the town and gown has become one of town versus gown due to the fact that many of those living within the communities around Harvard, at best, never experience the resources of the university. While at worst, these residents are negatively impacted through rising housing prices, a loss of the essential character of their community, and have to front the cost of the university’s consumption of public service due to Harvard refusing to pay their PILOT contributions and not providing meaningful community services in lieu of them either.

The narrative of Harvard’s expansion though is not entirely black-and-white, especially given how the history of Harvard’s expansion into Harvard Square, Cambridge, and now Allston often overshadows the efforts the university makes to help these communities. Harvard has an equally long history of providing aid and other programs to residents, including home ownership programs in Allston,43 running homeless shelters in Harvard Square, running a flex fund to improve public spaces,44 and partnering with Boston public schools among many other charitable initiatives.45

The detrimental effects of Harvard’s expansion are a result of an imbalance of power between the university and the communities surrounding it. There lacks a fundamental partnership between the university and its towns, arguably due to Harvard’s unwillingness to relinquish their power over the community – the one action needed to truly partner.

The university is aware of the negative effects it has on these communities, yet continually decides to remain unchanged. This is why many of the efforts Harvard makes to improve communities can be seen as reparations for the very problems Harvard has caused. These actions do not deepen Harvard’s ties with the greater community, rather, they only absolve Harvard from engaging with it on equal terms.

In order to create true change an equal partnership would need to emerge, which requires thinking creatively and redefining traditional approaches. Various community groups have already spoken on what Harvard can do to create this. For example, the PILOT Action Group has suggested the following terms to remedy the system:


“Full payment of PILOT from nonprofits; PILOT oversight commission with community representation; full transparency in community benefits reporting; and community engagement process for community benefits.”46

Harvard could also adopt the pledges it made for affordable housing, transportation, and professional development for the community within the ERC, to all of its proposals. As efforts like these work towards a more balanced partnership, local governments will also need to rethink the way Harvard is being held accountable in the meantime.

Whether such a partnership is attainable, or even favorable for these communities, remains an open question.
