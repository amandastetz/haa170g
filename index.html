Town Versus Gown?

Historically, the phrase “the town and gown” has been used to describe the two distinct communities living within a university town.


The “town” being the community’s non-academic residents and the “gown” being all those associated with the university – students, facility, and staff, amongst others.

Fittingly, Town Gown is the name of the report Harvard, MIT, Lesley University, and Cambridge College each release annually to the public to describe what they have been doing for the past year.1 It also describes what their future plans are regarding topics like land ownership, student and staff statistics, building renovations and construction, and energy consumption. Harvard University has called the report a “snapshot of Harvard’s presence in Cambridge”.2

The following data story is inspired by this town-gown relationship as it applies to Harvard University and the communities surrounding it: Harvard Square, greater-Cambridge, Allston, and more. It uses data from various Harvard Town Gown reports, Harvard GIS data, and other open-source databases, to look at what occurs when the “town and gown” relationship becomes one of “town versus gown”.

So, what does town versus gown look like, and why is this relationship relevant? To understand, it is first important to recognize Harvard’s growing physical presence, how it affects the community around it, and the community’s response to it.

As seen below, Harvard’s presence beyond its main campus has been slowly expanding as well since 2002.


As of 2018, Harvard has owned 9.56% of the land in Cambridge and 0.27% in Boston.3

The University currently owns land in Massachusetts, Maine, Connecticut, Washington D.C., and Wyoming. It also maintains an international presence through centers in Italy, Greece, and Turkey. In total, these holdings account for 25.8 million square feet of building space, and 5669 acres of land as of 2021.4

While the university’s expansion may not be negative in-and-of-itself, its expansion does not occur in a vacuum.


The communities Harvard is most immediately expanding into – Cambridge and Allston – are densely populated with little land available, meaning Harvard’s expansion is a zero-sum game: for the university to acquire new property, people, often entire communities, must be driven out. And those that stay, often do not remain for long.

Here the distinction between the town and gown is most apparent, because the residents of these communities the university seeps into do not benefit from its wealth nor the resources it provides internally. Rather, the immediate effects of Harvard’s expansion come in the form of skyrocketing property values and costs of living, which are impossible to keep up with on a stagnant income.5

"In the last 10 years, Cambridge has experienced some of the highest home appreciation rates of any community in the nation."6

Local residents and businesses are effectively priced out of communities they may have spent decades in. Yet, this story is not new.

In fact, Harvard has been in a state of continuous growth for over a century. To secure and maintain its property holdings at this rate, Harvard has drawn from the same playbook for nearly 400 years.

Understanding the evolving town-gown relationship will help make sense of the current relationship between Harvard Square, great-Cambridge, Allston, and Harvard.

Post-Allston: 1997 - Today
The Boston Globe called it,
“a stealthy land grab.”

Harvard called it,
“normal.”

In 1997, Harvard University disclosed that for the previous nine years they had spent nearly $88 million buying 52 acres of land in Allston, Massachusetts behind Harvard Business School and Harvard’s athletic complexes. Echoing the actions of almost a century earlier, the process had been conducted secretly under the name of an unaffiliated private real estate company, meaning the university’s identity was unknown to sellers, Allston residents, and local government officials.

Harvard claims to have done so out of necessity, stating that if they went through with the purchases under the university name, the prices would have been “overly inflated beyond market value.” Allston residents, on the other hand, viewed the terms of the purchases as deception.

Residents did not want the university further encroaching on their community, as it typically translates to a higher cost of living, less resources for residents, and greater gentrification. To mitigate these tensions, Harvard promised to remain “very sensitive to community interests” when it came to deciding the use of said properties.

It has been 25 years since this Boston Globe article was published, and Harvard now owns approximately ⅓ of the land in Allston. 6

This land has since been turned into a Science and Engineering Complex, an Innovation Lab, and Art Lab, along with other academic buildings, and these are just the start. As Harvard rolls out its plan for the remodeling of more of its Allston properties, it claims once again for these projects to be in service of the community. However, the reality of the situation is not so simple, especially with mounting opposition from neighborhood groups.

The “Allston Question” is perhaps the most symbolic of Harvard’s contemporary relationship with the communities it inhabits. The remainder of this data story will focus on Harvard’s presence in Allston to outline the current effects of expansion.

Alternatives: Viewpoints and Solutions

The "leaky pipeline" is a now common metaphor used to describe how women and other historically marginalized groups frequently exit STEM studies and careers. The metaphor is also used to explain why there are so few women occupying highly credentialed STEM occupations such as professorships or research positions.


The metaphor is as follows: if water (women) flows through a pipeline with cracks, much of the water will leak out (women exiting STEM) and a significantly smaller amount of water will remain at the end of the pipeline. In this metaphor, the starting and ending points of the pipeline vary, but what remains the same is that the pipeline itself marks the process of attaining higher and higher levels of STEM involvement – for example, the path from graduating with a bachelor's degree, to a master's degree, to a doctorate.

The leaky pipeline metaphor often serves as a teaching tool. It is a clear way to visualize women's diminishing representation as credentials increase. The metaphor invites inquiry into who it is that "leaks" from the pipeline, where the pipeline leaks, and how to prevent leakage. These types of questions have caused many policy solutions regarding increasing women's involvement in STEM to focus on educational benchmarks, such as early math and science education.1
The tepid success2 of interventions like these has caused this metaphor, and the questions it posits to be important, to receive several critiques: placing too much focus on benchmarks, placing the entirety of the "blame" on the women themselves, and assuming a universal experience in STEM, to name a few.

Only 15% of CS faculty positions in U.S. colleges are held by women.3


The following data story will use a variety of datasets from the U.S. Department of Education, the National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics, and the National Survey of College Graduates (2019), amongst others, to showcase some of these main critiques of the leaky pipeline metaphor. In doing so it will adopt an alternative perspective on how to improve retention of women in computer science.
